# HFH Website design brief

# 01 Context & Objectives

Based on the live site*: [humanfuturehub.org](http://humanfuturehub.org/)*

---

### About HFH

Human Future Hub is a Berlin-based AI literacy social enterprise in the process of registering as a gUG. Our mission: *empower humans to thrive in the AI era — nobody is left behind.* We operate across two strategic pillars — Future of Education and Future of Work — and build community through events, a newsletter, and an active LinkedIn group.

### Positioning

> “AI realists, not evangelists.”
> 
> 
> The emotional and psychological dimensions of AI disruption are our edge. We discuss systemic human impact — not product hype, not political commentary.
> 

### Why the site needs to change

The current site has four pages: Home, Events, About, Contact. It has no structure reflecting the two work pillars, no team or contributor pages, and the homepage leads with generic community framing rather than HFH’s distinct positioning. As HFH grows toward its gUG registration, formal partnerships, and funding applications, the site needs to reflect what the organisation actually does — and build credibility for institutional audiences.

| Objective | Rationale |
| --- | --- |
| Reflect the two pillars | Give Future of Education and Future of Work dedicated pages that can be linked to in outreach and grant applications. |
| Build institutional credibility | Team and Expert Contributors pages show funders and partners who is behind HFH and who advises it. |
| Surface the newsletter | Substack is the depth layer. It needs a proper landing page, not a footer link. |
| Fix the homepage framing | Lead with positioning, not community invitation. Three clear entry paths: Education, Work, Community. |
| Scale for Munich | Events page needs chapter filtering. Munich is now active. |

## 03 Content Positioning

These are content-level recommendations — use them to inform layout decisions, not as layout specifications.

### Home

The homepage is currently the weakest page. It leads with a community invitation and a wall of descriptive text that buries the positioning. Three changes are needed:

**Lead with positioning, not welcome.** The first thing a visitor reads should state what HFH is and why it’s different. “AI realists, not evangelists” is the line. It earns attention in a saturated space.

**Three entry paths, not one.** The homepage should visibly offer three paths — Future of Education, Future of Work, Community — so teachers, HR managers, and curious individuals each see something for them within seconds.

**Remove redundant copy.** The current page has significant duplication across sections. The homepage should be shorter, sharper, and trust the internal pages to carry the detail.

### Future of Education

This is the flagship pillar and the most fundable programme. The page needs to do three things: establish the problem credibly, explain HFH’s differentiated approach, and create a clear path for schools and educators to take action.

**The market gap is the hook.** No European player combines teachers and parents in the same community. The parent dimension is universally underserved. This is the differentiated claim — it should appear near the top, not buried.

**Name the programme.** Educate the Educator needs to be named and described on this page. Workshop structure, who it’s for, what participants leave with. Specificity builds trust.

**Two distinct audiences, one page.** Teachers and parents have different concerns. The page should acknowledge both explicitly without splitting into two pages at this stage.

**Use “AI literacy”, not “ethical AI”.** “AI literacy” is the fundable framing. “Ethical AI” is saturated. Language on this page should consistently use “AI literacy”, “responsible use”, and “practical guidance” over abstract ethical framing.

### Future of Work

This page addresses organisations, HR leaders, and teams navigating AI implementation. The tone shifts slightly — more B2B, more outcome-focused — while staying within HFH’s “realist” positioning.

**Lead with the organisational problem.** Companies are implementing AI without governance, without preparing their people, and without addressing the anxiety it creates. HFH sits at the intersection of these problems. The page should name this before describing what HFH offers.

**Describe what’s available.** Expert sessions, advisory, community access. Keep this concrete and short — institutional audiences want to know what they’re getting, not read a manifesto.

**Separate individual and organisational framing.** The homepage is for individuals navigating change. This page is for people making decisions on behalf of organisations. The copy should reflect that shift without losing the human tone.

### Team

The Team page builds trust for all audiences — funders, partners, prospective contributors, and anyone wondering who is behind the organisation. It is especially important at the gUG registration stage.

**Names, roles, cities — in that order.** Don’t bury the human in a title. Name first. Role second. City shows the geographic spread and reinforces the distributed, global-first positioning.

**Short bios that say something real.** Two or three lines. What someone’s background is and why they joined HFH. No boilerplate (“passionate about AI” adds nothing). The bios should sound like people wrote them.

**Volunteer context, not apology.** HFH is volunteer-run. Frame it as a strength — a group of people who chose to build this — not a limitation. A “join the team” CTA fits naturally here.

### Expert Contributors

This page serves two purposes: it builds HFH’s credibility externally and acts as a recruitment funnel for new advisors.

**Domain tags, not long bios.** Expert cards should be scannable: name, field, affiliation, and one or two domain tags (Education, Psychology, Future of Work, Tech, Policy). Lengthy bios on this page slow the scan.

**The ask should be visible.** “Become an expert advisor” needs to appear on this page, not just be accessible via Contact. HFH is especially looking for professionals in education, psychology, sociology, and recruiting.

**Show the network, not the hierarchy.** Advisors are peers, not staff. The framing should be “our network includes” not “our team has”. This reinforces HFH’s community model.

## 04 Voice Guidelines

### Five core principles

| Principle | What it means |
| --- | --- |
| Warm but not fluffy | Serious topics, human approach. Empathy comes through specificity, not filler phrases like “we care deeply”. |
| Punchy and direct | Short sentences earn their place. So do long ones, when they need to land. Avoid padding. |
| Peer-to-peer, not broadcast | We’re thinking alongside people, not talking at them. Direct address: “you”, “we”, not “our community members”. |
| Specific over generic | “Teachers in Germany have no structured AI support” beats “AI is changing education”. Name the real situation. |
| Honest, not sugar-coated | Don’t minimise difficulty. We don’t promise what we can’t deliver. Realistic optimism over hype. |

### What to avoid

| Pattern | Why |
| --- | --- |
| Rhythmic three-part structures | “We educate. We connect. We empower.” — sounds like it was written by a committee. |
| Manufactured warmth | “Passionate about”, “dedicated to”, “committed to excellence” — these phrases are empty. |
| AI-era boilerplate | “In today’s rapidly evolving world”, “as AI transforms everything” — skip the premise, say the thing. |
| Nonprofit clichés | “Together we can”, “making a difference”, “changing lives” — HFH’s voice is more grounded than this. |
| Hype or product commentary | No celebrating AI capabilities. No reactions to specific model launches. Stay systemic. |

### Self-check

Read the copy aloud. Does it sound like a committee wrote it? Rewrite. Does it start with the strongest idea? If not, restructure. Would a thoughtful peer find this useful and true? That’s the bar.

---

### Visual principles

- Human imagery over tech clichés — real people and contexts. No circuit boards, robot hands, or blue brain graphics.
- Clean, uncluttered layouts — white space is doing work. One strong visual idea per section.
- Colour as signal, not decoration — Blue anchors, Teal supports, Orange highlights.
- Typography does the lifting — when copy is strong, design can be minimal.

### Positioning guardrails

- **Systemic, not reactive** — AI’s human impact on work, society, education, values. Not individual product launches.
- **Realist, not evangelist** — No hype. No uncritical celebration of AI. Emotional and psychological dimensions are the edge.
- **Human first** — Technology is context. The human experience is the subject.
- **No political commentary** — No specific current political events. Systemic discussion only.
